Mobile robot navigation fundamentally depends on understanding the kinematic constraints of the robot platform. Differential-drive robots, such as the TurtleBot3, operate under nonholonomic kinematic constraints, meaning they cannot move freely in all directions at a single instant. Instead, their motion is restricted to movements that are physically achievable through wheel rotations.

In differential-drive robots, we essentially drive the movements of two wheels on (one on each side of) the chassis that are driven independently by two motors. That is because the coordinated actuation of said wheels determines the linear velocity of the robot v and the angular velocity of the robot, which is 0. These input commands have a direct impact on the pose of the robot, its position and orientation in a global frame. The kinematic correlation between the inputs of the velocity and the change of pose is given as:

\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix}
\dot{x} \\
\dot{y} \\
\dot{\theta}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
v \cos(\theta) \\
v \sin(\theta) \\
\omega
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item $x, y$ represent the robot's position in the global coordinate frame
\item $\theta$ represents the robot's heading angle (orientation)
\item $\dot{x}, \dot{y}$ represent linear velocity components along the x and y axes
\item $v$ represents linear velocity
\item $\omega$ represents angular velocity
\end{itemize}

These kinematic constraints limit the robot's ability to execute arbitrary motion trajectories. Consequently, real world navigation needs to smooth path, plan trajectory and offer proper motion control measures to have viable and steady movement of a robot.

\subsection{Trajectory Smoothing and Interpolation}

Raw paths generated by discrete planning algorithms, such as grid-based or sampling-based planners, often contain sharp turns, discontinuous curvature, and kinematic infeasibilities.The above anomalies present serious difficulties to the direct implementation of differential-drive robots that are required to follow the smooth and continuous motion profiles and comply with their nonholonomic constraints. To overcome these constraints, they use trajectory smoothing to turn discrete paths represented as waypoints into continuous and differentiable paths with the use of waypoints as prongs of a path representation ~\cite{ravankar2018path}. This process uses the interpolation methods to create smooth curves, which simultaneously meet the velocity conditions, deceleration conditions, and curvature conditions that are necessary to achieve a stable robotic movement. Removal of the sudden directional changes provided by the trajectory smoothing leads to a better tracking precision, less mechanical forces on the actuators, and increased efficiency of the navigation in general. 

Interpolation methods based on spline interpolations are widely used in smoothing of trajectories due to its natural flexibility and mathematical soundness. Among such variants, the most common are cubic splines and B-splines, which are found in this class. Cubic splines produce piecewise-linear curves that ensure continuity in position, velocity and acceleration of one segment to the next; that they move directly to the known waypoints and that there are smooth transitions between consecutive path segments. B-splines on the other hand offer a higher degree of flexibility, since they do not strictly interpolate the waypoints but approximate them. The nature of this allows B- splines to produce more smooth trajectories with finite curvature which is especially beneficial when motion tracking controllers are used in mobile robots. The use of spline-based smoothing makes the corresponding trajectory dynamically realistic and capable of execution by control algorithms, which in turn can be used by differential-drive robots when following planned paths safely and efficiently.

\textbf{Natural Cubic Spline Interpolation for Path Smoothing}

Path smoothing is essential in mobile robot navigation because discrete planning algorithms such as A* and RRT generate piecewise-linear trajectories with sharp directional changes. These non-circular trajectories are in contradiction with the kinematic limitations of the differential-drive robots, which causes sudden and jerky movement, reduced accuracy in tracking and increased mechanical fatigue. Trajectory-smoothing methods are used to transform discrete sequence of waypoints into continuous curves which are kinematically allowable to be actually executed by a robot through smooth and continuously differentiable curves.

Natural cubic splines are widely used as interpolation techniques in robotics because of their smooth nature, computational efficiency and the inherent ease of interpolating. A natural cubic spline is a discontinuous cubic polynomial that goes through each of the specified waypoints and applies continuity of the first and second derivatives at all continuity points.

These derivative conditions ensure that the resulting trajectory has smooth velocity and acceleration profiles which are essential in the realization of stable and compliant motion control.

\textbf{Mathematical Definition of Natural Cubic Splines}
The mathematical formulation of natural cubic splines follows standard numerical 
analysis approaches~\cite{siciliano2016springer}.
Given a sequence of n + 1 waypoints $(x_0, y_0), (x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$, a natural cubic spline $S(t)$ consists of n cubic polynomial segments:

\begin{equation}
S_i(t) = a_i + b_i(t - t_i) + c_i(t - t_i)^2 + d_i(t - t_i)^3, \quad t \in [t_i, t_{i+1}]
\end{equation}

where $i = 0, 1, \ldots, n - 1$, and $t$ is a parameter (typically arc length or normalized distance).

The spline coefficients $(a_i, b_i, c_i, d_i)$ are determined by enforcing the following constraints:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{Interpolation:} The spline passes through all waypoints:
\begin{align}
S_i(t_i) &= y_i \quad \text{and} \quad S_i(t_{i+1}) = y_{i+1}
\end{align}

\item \textbf{$C^1$ Continuity:} First derivatives are continuous at interior points:
\begin{equation}
S_i'(t_{i+1}) = S_{i+1}'(t_{i+1}), \quad i = 0, 1, \ldots, n - 2
\end{equation}

\item \textbf{$C^2$ Continuity:} Second derivatives are continuous at interior points:
\begin{equation}
S_i''(t_{i+1}) = S_{i+1}''(t_{i+1}), \quad i = 0, 1, \ldots, n - 2
\end{equation}

\item \textbf{Natural Boundary Conditions:} Second derivatives at the endpoints are zero:
\begin{equation}
S_0''(t_0) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad S_{n-1}''(t_n) = 0
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

These constraints produce a system of $(n+1)$ linear equations that can be efficiently solved using tridiagonal matrix methods. The natural boundary condition ensures that the resulting curve is the ``smoothest'' possible interpolation in terms of minimizing total curvature energy.

\textbf{Parametric Representation for Robot Path Planning}
For mobile robot trajectories in 2D space, a parametric representation is used instead of the standard $y = f(x)$ form. In parametric form, both x and y coordinates are expressed as functions of an independent parameter $t$:

\begin{equation}
P(t) = \begin{bmatrix} x(t) \\ y(t) \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

where $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ are separate natural cubic splines fitted to the waypoint coordinates.

\textbf{Why Parametric Representation?}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{Handles Vertical Segments:} Standard splines require monotonically increasing x values, which fails for vertical or looping paths. Parametric splines avoid this restriction.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Related Technology 1 -- Path Planning Algorithms}

Path planning is a fundamental problem in autonomous mobile robotics, involving the computation of a collision-free and efficient path from a start location to a target within a known or partially known environment. The quality of the path planner directly affects navigation safety, execution smoothness, and real-time performance. Numerous algorithms have been proposed in the literature, each offering different trade-offs between optimality, computational cost, and adaptability to dynamic environments. This section reviews classical and modern path planning algorithms and justifies the selection of the Improved A* (IA*) algorithm for this project.

\subsubsection{A* Algorithm}

The A* algorithm is a widely used graph-based search method~ that finds the shortest path on a discretised map using the evaluation function:

\begin{equation}
f(n) = g(n) + h(n)
\end{equation}

where $g(n)$ is the accumulated cost from the start node to node $n$, and $h(n)$ is a heuristic estimate of the remaining cost from $n$ to the goal. When the heuristic is admissible (i.e., it never overestimates the true cost), A* guarantees an optimal solution. In indoor grid maps, Euclidean or Manhattan distance heuristics are typically employed\cite{zhang2022improved}.

Despite its optimality guarantee, A* produces grid-locked paths with sharp angular turns that are kinematically difficult for differential-drive robots to follow directly. The raw A* output is therefore unsuitable for direct execution and requires further post-processing before deployment on a physical or simulated robot platform.

\subsubsection{Improved A* (IA*) Algorithm}

The Improved A* (IA*) algorithm extends standard A* by addressing its limitations in robotic navigation contexts~\cite{wang2021mobile}. The three key enhancements introduced in IA* are:
\begin{itemize}
\item Diagonal Motion Support -- Enables movement in eight directions instead of four, resulting in shorter and more geometrically natural paths on occupancy grid maps.
\item Obstacle Inflation -- Obstacles are virtually expanded by a configurable safety margin prior to planning, ensuring the robot maintains adequate clearance from walls and physical objects throughout navigation.
\item Waypoint Pruning -- Redundant intermediate waypoints are removed using line-of-sight checks between non-adjacent nodes, producing a compact and clean waypoint set that significantly reduces downstream computational overhead.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Justification for Selecting Improved A*}

Based on the comparison presented in Table~\ref{tab:path_planning_comparison}, the Improved A* algorithm was selected as the global path planner for this project for the following reasons. Dijkstra's algorithm was eliminated due to its exhaustive node exploration, making it computationally impractical on large-resolution indoor maps. Standard A* was deemed insufficient because its jagged grid-locked output requires significant post-processing and lacks the safety margin and pruning capabilities built into IA*. Sampling-based planners such as RRT and RRT* were rejected due to their high computational cost, non-deterministic behaviour, and irregular path outputs that are unsuitable for structured 2D indoor environments. D* Lite, while well-suited for dynamic replanning, introduces unnecessary implementation complexity since dynamic obstacle avoidance in this project is managed at the local planner level using real-time sensor data. Potential Field methods were disqualified due to their well-known susceptibility to local minima traps in corridor-based layouts.

IA* satisfies all key requirements of the proposed system: it operates directly on the SLAM-generated occupancy grid map, guarantees a near-optimal collision-free path, enforces safety clearance through obstacle inflation, and produces a compact pruned waypoint set that integrates seamlessly with the cubic spline smoothing and Model Predictive Control pipeline described in the subsequent sections.

\subsection{Related Technology 2 -- Path Smoothing and Optimization Techniques}

\subsubsection{Overview}

Path smoothing is a critical post-processing step in mobile robot navigation that transforms piecewise-linear paths generated by discrete planners (such as A* or RRT) into continuous, kinematically feasible trajectories. While various smoothing approaches exist in the literature, this section reviews both classical interpolation methods and optimization-based techniques to contextualize the natural cubic spline approach adopted in this project.

\subsubsection{Classical Interpolation Approaches}

Cubic Spline Interpolation: As discussed in Section 2.1.3, natural cubic splines provide a deterministic, efficient method for generating $C^2$-continuous trajectories. They are widely used in industrial robotics due to their computational efficiency and guaranteed smoothness. The primary limitation is that they do not explicitly optimize for path length or curvature they simply interpolate through the given waypoints in the smoothest possible way.

B-Spline Approximation: Unlike cubic splines, B-splines approximate rather than interpolate waypoints, offering greater flexibility in shape control through adjustable control points. However, this flexibility comes at the cost of not guaranteeing passage through the original waypoints, which can compromise obstacle avoidance guarantees from the global planner. B-splines are more commonly used in CAD systems and freeform path design rather than waypoint-based robot navigation.~\cite{ravankar2018path,mercy2017spline}

\subsubsection{Optimization-Based Smoothing: PSO with Bezier Curves}

Recent research has explored the use of Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) combined with Bezier curves to generate optimized smooth paths~\cite{song2020smooth}. This approach treats path smoothing as an optimization problem where the positions of Bezier curve control points are the decision variables.

\textbf{How it Works:}

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item A high-degree Bezier curve (typically degree 6 or higher) is defined between the start and goal positions.
\item PSO is used to optimize the positions of the interior control points.
\item The fitness function minimizes:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item Total path length
\item Maximum curvature
\item Curvature derivative (jerk)
\item Collision penalties (distance to obstacles)
\end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

The resulting Bezier curve provides a smooth, optimized trajectory with guaranteed high-order continuity.

\textbf{Advantages:}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item Explicit optimization allows direct control over path length and curvature bounds.
\item High-degree Bezier curves naturally satisfy $G^3$-continuity without additional constraints.
\item PSO is derivative-free, making it suitable for non-convex optimization landscapes with obstacle constraints.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Disadvantages:}

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{Computational Cost:} PSO requires iterative evaluation of candidate solutions (typically 50-100 particles over 20-50 iterations), making it unsuitable for real-time replanning.

\item \textbf{Parameter Sensitivity:} PSO performance depends heavily on parameter tuning (inertia weight, cognitive/social coefficients).

\item \textbf{No Interpolation Guarantee:} The optimized Bezier curve may deviate significantly from the original A* waypoints, potentially violating obstacle clearance in complex environments.

\item \textbf{Convergence Uncertainty:} As a stochastic algorithm, PSO does not guarantee global optimality and may produce different results across runs.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Hybrid Approaches: Local PSO Optimization with Spline Smoothing}

Recognizing the complementary strengths of interpolation and optimization methods, this project adopts a hybrid approach:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{Global Path Planning:} Improved A* generates a collision-free waypoint sequence.
\item \textbf{Waypoint Pruning:} Line-of-sight checks remove redundant intermediate waypoints.
\item \textbf{Local PSO Refinement (Optional):} For selected waypoints in tight corridors, PSO can optimize their positions within a small radius to reduce local curvature while maintaining obstacle clearance.
\item \textbf{Natural Cubic Spline Smoothing:} The final (or pruned) waypoint sequence is smoothed using parametric cubic splines to generate the executable trajectory.
\end{enumerate}

This hybrid strategy balances the computational efficiency of spline interpolation with the potential for local optimization when needed. Importantly, the spline smoothing step is always performed, ensuring a kinematically feasible output regardless of whether PSO refinement is applied.

\subsubsection{Justification for Cubic Spline Selection}

For the autonomous parcel delivery application, natural cubic splines were selected over PSO-Bezier optimization for the following reasons:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{Real-Time Performance:} Indoor delivery robots require rapid replanning when obstacles appear. Cubic splines provide deterministic, fast smoothing suitable for dynamic environments.

\item \textbf{Obstacle Avoidance Guarantee:} By interpolating through A* waypoints, cubic splines preserve the collision-free guarantees of the global planner. Bezier optimization may introduce deviations that require additional validation.

\item \textbf{Integration with MPC:} The parametric representation and analytical derivatives of cubic splines integrate seamlessly with Model Predictive Control, enabling accurate trajectory tracking.

\item \textbf{Implementation Simplicity:} Using scipy.interpolate.CubicSpline reduces implementation complexity and improves code maintainability compared to implementing and tuning a PSO-Bezier optimizer.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Related Technology 3 -- Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) Based Path Planning}

The Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) is an evolutionary optimization algorithm that is based on population which finds numerous applications in robotics and autonomous navigation in finding solutions to nonlinear complex optimization problems~\cite{krell2019collision}. PSO is used to address the smooth path planning problem of mobile robots in which control points of continuous high-degree Bézier curves are optimized.

The article constitutes robot path planning as an optimization problem with several objectives like minimization of the path length, obstacle avoidance, a smooth curvature, and motion continuity being addressed together. The authors point out that traditional path planning methods tend to produce piecewise linear or polygonal-shaped paths that have sharp turns. These disjunctions lead to the result of frequent changing of motion states leading to unstable robot motion, high energy consumption as well as mechanical stress.~\cite{song2020smooth}.

In order to address these challenges, a better PSO algorithm is provided, which incorporates adaptive fractional-order velocity in the velocity update equation. In the classical PSO, personal best and global best solutions are used to update the particle position and velocity. The refined PSO however includes the historical velocity memory, which increases the capability of the global search and reduces the premature convergence.

The improved velocity update model is expressed as:

\begin{equation}
v_i^{k+1} = w \cdot v_i^k + c_1 \cdot r_1 \cdot (p_{\text{best},i} - x_i^k) + c_2 \cdot r_2 \cdot (g_{\text{best}} - x_i^k) + \alpha \sum_{j=1}^{m} \beta_j v_i^{k-j}
\end{equation}

where particle movement is influenced not only by current velocity but also by velocities from previous iterations. This fractional-order memory introduces controlled disturbances that help particles escape local minima and improve global exploration.

The optimization objective function minimizes path length, curvature, and curvature variation:

\begin{equation}
J = w_1 L + w_2 \int \kappa^2 ds + w_3 \int \left(\frac{d\kappa}{ds}\right)^2 ds
\end{equation}

This formulation ensures generation of smooth and dynamically feasible robot paths.

\subsubsection{Relevance to Our Project}

The PSO-based optimization system is very applicable to our mobile robot path planning project. Our project aims at the creation of collision-free smooth navigation paths through smart and intelligent planning algorithms that are incorporated into a web-based planner system.

The enhanced PSO method assists in optimization of smoothness of the path that is in direct relation with the path refinement and smoothing modules of our project. Moreover, the idea of addressing the path parameters optimization rather than the post-processing smoothing is used to complement our spline and smoothing solutions in the backend processing. Optimization based planning also contributes to dynamic adaptability important in real time generation and visualization of web based paths.

\subsubsection{Limitations Identified}

Although the enhanced PSO algorithm is efficient, a number of limitations exist. Additional computational complexity is also presented in the algorithm with the calculation of fractions-order velocity and adaptive parameter adjustment. This adds to the time of execution and it can reduce real-time performance in large or highly dynamic environments.

The approach also presupposes grid-like workspace, which is easier to represent obstacles with, but less applicable to irregular or unstructured setting. In addition, performance of optimization is also dependent on the choice of parameters like inertia weights and acceleration coefficients, which could need manual optimization according to the environment.

The other weakness is that the methodology is only tested based on simulation experiments instead of real robotic implementation. There is no discussion of practical implementation issues like noise of sensors, dynamic movement of obstacles and uncertainty in localization.

\subsection{Related Technology 4 -- Cellular Decomposition}

Cellular decomposition is a common place-planning approach and representation of the environment of classical environment representation in autonomous robot navigation~\cite{galceran2013survey}. The way splits the robot area into smaller areas with no overlaps, known as cells, which makes navigation much simpler as the continuous environment is represented in a structured format. It is a technique that enhances efficiency in computation and reduces collisions in addition to the systematic generation of paths.

Recent studies indicate that cellular decomposition is done by subdividing the free space into a finite number of smaller cells where:

\begin{equation}
W_{\text{free}} = \bigcup_{i=1}^{N} C_i
\end{equation}

where $C_i$ represents each decomposed navigable cell and $N$ is the total number of cells. Each cell is classified as either free or occupied based on obstacle presence. This decomposition allows path planning to be performed by navigating through adjacent free cells rather than analyzing the entire workspace.

Most modern approaches implement convex cellular decomposition, where each cell satisfies convexity conditions~\cite{choset2001coverage}. Convex cells ensure that any two points inside the cell can be connected using a straight-line path without intersecting obstacles. The feasibility condition for convex cells can be expressed as:

\begin{equation}
\forall p_1, p_2 \in C_i, \quad \lambda p_1 + (1-\lambda) p_2 \in C_i, \quad \lambda \in [0,1]
\end{equation}

This property significantly reduces path-planning complexity and improves collision detection efficiency.

Cellular decomposition also enables the formation of a connectivity graph, where each cell is represented as a node and adjacency between cells forms graph edges. The optimal navigation path is determined by minimizing traversal cost between connected cells. The cost function used in cell-based planning is typically defined as:

\begin{equation}
J = \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} d(C_i, C_{i+1}) + n \cdot c_{\text{transition}}
\end{equation}

where $d(C_i, C_{i+1})$ represents the distance between adjacent cell centers and $n$ denotes the number of transitions between cells.

Recent research has developed cellular decomposition incorporating it with a heuristic search algorithm like Astar and dynamic programming. Such hybrid methods enable efficient path generation over a global scale, by choosing the best cell transitions with the subpaths already calculated previously being stored in order to cut back on computational costs. Also, recent applications use adaptive decomposition schemes, where cell sizes adapt dynamically based on obstacle density, enhancing scalability and path optimality.

Among the most important benefits of cellular decomposition, simplification of complex environments into organized navigation areas can be identified, which makes it useful in indoor robotic applications. It is also an effective method of obstacle avoidance and it is easily combined with optimization and trajectory tracking algorithms like Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) and Model Predictive Control (MPC).

\textbf{Advantages of Cellular Decomposition}

\begin{itemize}
\item Simplifies complex environments into structured navigation regions
\item Reduces computational complexity by enabling graph-based path planning
\item Improves collision avoidance through structured obstacle representation
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Relevance with Our Project}

The cellular decomposition is directly related to the suggested autonomous navigation system since it offers an organized outline of the robot workspace. The environment in this project has been segregated into grid cells to differentiate between the free space and the obstacle areas. This decomposition is also useful in global path planning since such algorithms as A* and Dynamic Programming can now be used to search the connected free cells, rather than searching the entire continuous space. It is also aimed at bettering collision detection, aiding efficient map generation in web-based planner and optimizing paths and tracking trajectories with PSO and MPC through the ROS2-Gazebo simulation platform.

\subsubsection{Limitations of Cellular Decomposition}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{High Computational Cost:} Fine grid decomposition increases memory usage and computational complexity.
\item \textbf{Resolution Dependency:} Large cells reduce planning accuracy, while small cells increase processing time.
\item \textbf{Difficulty in Dynamic Environments:} Frequent environment changes require repeated decomposition and replanning.
\item \textbf{Path Suboptimality:} Grid-based decomposition may produce longer or less smooth paths compared to continuous-space planning methods.
\item \textbf{Scalability Issues:} Performance decreases when applied to very large or highly cluttered environments.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Related Technology 6 -- ROS2 Navigation Stack (NAV2 Framework)}

The ROS2 Navigation Stack (NAV2)~\cite{macenski2020marathon,macenski2022nav2} provides a complete solution for autonomous robot navigation. NAV2 includes modules for localization, mapping, path planning, and motion control. Localization is commonly achieved using Adaptive Monte Carlo Localization (AMCL), which estimates robot pose using LiDAR data and a known map. Mapping is performed using SLAM algorithms to generate occupancy grid maps. Path planning in NAV2 consists of global planners for long-term routes and local planners for obstacle avoidance. Motion control modules generate velocity commands for safe navigation. Both academic and industrial implementations emphasize the importance of sensor integration, particularly LiDAR and IMU data, for reliable navigation pipelines.

\subsection{Related Technology 7 -- Visual SLAM vs LiDAR-Based SLAM}

SLAM systems are commonly categorized based on the primary sensor used for perception: visual sensors (cameras) or LiDAR sensors. Visual SLAM systems rely on monocular, stereo or RGB-D cameras to derive features and obtain depth data~\cite{campos2021orb}. They offer high appearance at low cost and are ideal in indoor environment conditions in which there are texture and lighting cues. However, they are sensitive to light effects and change of scene. In contrast, LiDAR-based SLAM is a process which employs laser scanners to create 3D point clouds, which do not depend on lighting conditions~\cite{li2021indoor}. LiDAR does not have, even though it is able to offer very accurate geometrical measurements. Recent deep learning-based visual SLAM prove that systems based on cameras can be accurate in the same way as LiDAR at the same time as they are cost-effective in hardware, which makes them ideal in indoor autonomous delivery robots.

\subsection{Related Technology 8 -- DROID-SLAM Framework and Dense Bundle Adjustment}

DROID-SLAM~\cite{teed2021droid} presents a deep learning-driven visual SLAM framework capable of operating with monocular, stereo, and RGB-D cameras. The system utilizes a recurrent neural architecture that does a sequential optimization of camera pose and dense depth maps. Another important innovation is the application of the Dense Bundle Adjustment (DBA) in a fully differentiable pipeline. In contrast to classical SLAM which executes sparse bundle adjustment, DROID-SLAM has a joint optimization of pixel-wise depth and camera poses across multiple frames. This repetition optimization does help a great deal to diminish drift and improves tracking strength which is particularly good in low-texture indoor places like walls and floors. Also, the framework makes generalizations across sensor modalities, without re-generalizing training. In the case of autonomous parcel delivery robots, the DROID-SLAM facilitates high levels of acculocalize rates with the help of a low-cost RGB or RGB-D camera alone.

\subsection{Related Technology 9 -- MAGiC-SLAM Multi-Agent Gaussian Mapping}

MAGiC-SLAM~\cite{yugay2024magic} introduces a multi-agent SLAM framework based on a 3D Gaussian scene representation. Rather than describing the environment as the point clouds or voxels, the model is represented by Gaussian primitives that enable the representation of the scene in an efficient manner, and map merging. The most important contribution of MAGiC-SLAM is that it is capable of fusing maps formed by several robots into a map on a global scale by the means of trajectory alignment and loop closure. This method enhances large indoor en-scalability, vironments and minimizes drift. In a place where numerous delivery robots are used at the same time, e.g. warehouses or hospitals, MAGiC-SLAM provides a viable tool of collaborative mapping and common space understanding.

\subsection{Related Technology 10 -- WildGS-SLAM for Dynamic Environment Mapping}

WildGS-SLAM~\cite{zheng2025wildgs} addresses the challenge of SLAM in dynamic environments using a monocular camera and Gaussian splatting representation. Indoor delivery environments often include moving humans, doors which open and close and moving objects that are capable of corrupting the SLAM map. WildGS-SLAM proposes an uncertain tracking and mapping filter which identifies and eliminates dynamical features in the scene without the use of semantic segmentation or depth sensors. Through uncertainty prediction per-pixel, integrating it with dense bundle adjustment and Gaussian maps optimization, WildGS-SLAM performs a good tracking and high-fidelity mapping in dense indoor environments. This feature is very applicable in the parcel delivery robots which act in real-life enhighly moving and occluding environments.

\section{Related Studies / Research}

Various research studies have explored various issues of autonomous mobile robot navigation that will aid the design and development of the proposed indoor parcel delivery robot system.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Hybrid Path Planning Strategies:} Comparative studies highlight the effectiveness of combining classical graph-based algorithms such as A* with optimization and learning-based approaches~\cite{ajeil2020grid}. Research demonstrates that hybrid navigation improves path optimality, adaptability, and computational efficiency.

\item \textbf{Path Smoothing and Trajectory Optimization:} Research on trajectory smoothing techniques indicates that raw paths often contain sharp turns and discontinuities~\cite{ravankar2018path}. Studies show that spline-based interpolation and curve-fitting methods improve motion smoothness, reduce mechanical stress, and enhance tracking accuracy.

\item \textbf{PSO-Based Path Optimization:} Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) has been widely investigated for optimizing robot trajectories~\cite{song2020smooth}. Research shows that PSO can minimize path length, curvature variation, and energy consumption. Improved PSO variants demonstrate faster convergence and enhanced optimization performance.

\item \textbf{Dynamic Obstacle Avoidance in Indoor Environments:} Human-aware navigation research emphasizes integrating vision-based perception systems with real-time obstacle avoidance algorithms~\cite{kumar2024real}. Studies demonstrate that combining computer vision models with adaptive motion planning improves safety and reliability.

\item \textbf{ROS2-Based Autonomous Navigation Frameworks:} Several studies have evaluated ROS2 middleware for autonomous robotic applications~\cite{hwang2023ros2}. These studies demonstrate that ROS2 provides modular architecture, real-time communication support, and improved scalability.

\item \textbf{SLAM Performance in Indoor Navigation:} Comparative research on visual SLAM, LiDAR-based SLAM, and deep learning-based SLAM techniques indicates that visual SLAM provides cost-effective localization~\cite{rosinol2020kimera}.

\item \textbf{Model Predictive Control for Mobile Robots:} Research on MPC demonstrates its ability to handle motion constraints, optimize trajectory tracking, and improve stability~\cite{nascimento2023nonlinear}. MPC-based controllers provide improved performance compared to classical PID controllers.

\item \textbf{Simulation-Based Navigation Testing:} Studies on simulation environments highlight their importance in validating robot navigation algorithms before real-world deployment~\cite{park2021simulation}. Simulation platforms~\cite{koenig2004gazebo} allow safe testing of SLAM, obstacle avoidance, and path planning strategies.
\end{itemize}

The detailed knowledge of these bottlenecks is invaluable to stipulate the areas in which a better navigation strategy and system architecture are justified.

\section{Problem Statement}

Even though the current robotic middleware applications like ROS 2 and the Navigation 2 (Nav2) architecture offer good baseline navigation functions, a number of technical challenges remain. Delivery of parcels manually in large indoor areas is time-consuming, labor intensive and prone to delays. The majority of the current implementations are dependent on pre-configured navigation modules which restricts flexibility of the system. Existing autonomous delivery solutions have severe constraints, including path jagging which breaks the kinematic limit of differentially-driven robots. The additional effects of path smoothing and trajectory optimization are unstable in robot motion. Moreover, a lot of implementations cannot deal with dynamic obstacle avoidance. Localization accuracy is also a major problem, especially when it comes to visual static or dynamic environments. More so, traditional methods of motion control like fixed-gain controllers demand a lot of parameter adjustment. To address these challenges, this project will provide an integrated solution with ROS 2 middleware-based robot that encompasses effective SLAM-based localization and hybrid path planning.
